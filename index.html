Causes of Global Warming

Source: Our World In Data

Ever since the Industrial Revolution, energy usage has grown exponentially. Higher-grade fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas replaced cheap biomass fuels (such as timber and peat) since they produce higher energy yields.

While coal, oil, and natural gas are great for producing energy, they harm our environment. These three sources of energy, known as fossil fuels, are notorious for releasing vast amounts of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. Our forests cannot pull carbon dioxide out of the air as fast as it accumulates.

As carbon dioxide accumulates into the atmosphere, more infrared radiation is reflected to the Earth, causing substantial temperature increases. As the climate increases, a phenomenon known as global warming occurs, causing water loss, which may lead to species loss and unintentional forest fires.

Source: Carbon Dioxide

Carbon dioxide, the most common greenhouse gas, has increased exponentially in the past couple of years, causing global warming.

Contrary to fossil fuel burning for power, clear-cutting and slash-and-burn techniques are another cause of global warming. These techniques, known as deforestation, remove or burn forests to make room for agriculture, urbanization, or landfills. Deforestation is a massive problem because it removes natural carbon dioxide sequesters (plants and trees) and releases more carbon dioxide into the atmosphere from timber combustion.

If everyone worked harder to become more energy sustainable, we could reverse the adverse side effects of global warming. Using energy more efficiently and only when needed would reduce the usage of fossil fuels, lessening the amount of greenhouse gases produced that reflect solar radiation to Earth. Furthermore, if we practiced more waste-reduction techniques such as composting, we could decrease deforestation as fewer landfills are required. Together, we can all stop global warming.

Local Solutions

Source: Photovoltaic Cell

The best solutions include switching to renewable energy sources such as solar photovoltaic cells (solar energy), ground-source heat pumps (geothermal energy), or tidal energy (water power); however, some of these solutions are too expensive for the typical person to switch to.

Depending on the household, the most common strategies being implemented for increasing sustainability include:

  Less Lighting

  • This may seem obvious, but people still accidentally leave their lights on at night.

  Less Heating

  • Natural gas is the most common fossil fuel for heating. Heating is costly (in terms of electricity). It also provides only a temporary effect (as heat dissipates quickly in buildings).
  • So the next time you turn the thermostat up, consider if you need it. Try wearing a jacket or sweater or taking a quick warm shower instead.

  Less Meat

  • Meat requires on average 16 times more energy to produce compared to crops. Reducing your intake of crops will reduce the consumption of energy-intensive foods.

  More Public Transportation

  • Compared to driving alone, Public transportation reduces greenhouse gas production by magnitudes from less gas consumed per person.
  • Use buses, trains, and carpools.

  More Green Structures

  • Building green roofs can increase albedo (reflection of sunlight) while providing shade and reducing overall air temperatures (reducing energy required for air conditioning).
  • Building rain gardens can help collect rainwater, preventing excess runoff into storm drains and also improve the quality of life.

  More Recycling / Composting

  • Recycling glass bottles, paper, or some forms of plastic allows them to be reused rather than end up in landfills, reducing the overall waste produced.
  • Composting prevents food scraps or other compostable materials from ending up in landfill, where they will take longer to decompose. Composted material can act as garden soil and fertilizer.

Overall, here are some of the simple solutions you can implement to increase sustainability. However, globally, we must work to seek more long-term solutions to address the damages we have already caused.

Global Solutions

This is not a joke. You know what the Joint Chiefs told us the greatest threat facing America was? Global warming.
Source: Remarks by President Biden to U.S. Air Force Personnel and Families Stationed at Royal Air Force Mildenhall

Long-term solutions include countries switching to more renewable sources of energy. While renewable sources of energy have their pros and cons, they do not deplete fossil fuel reserves and do not release greenhouse gases.

The three most used renewable sources of energy today include:

  • Solar Power
  • Hydro Power
  • Wind Power

Benefits and Cons of Solar, Hydro, and Wind Power

Solar Power

  Benefit   Con
No air pollutants (PM, SOx, NOx) released to generate electricity Semiconductor metals (silicon) need to be mined to produced PV cells (solar panels)
No CO2 released when generating electricity Silicon is a limited resource
No mining of fossil fuels for electricity production Solar Panel farms can displace habitats
Not always active (intermittency)

Hydro Power

  Benefit   Con
No greenhouse gas emissions when producing electricity Reservoir floods behind dam
Reservoir and dam can be tourist attractions Fossil fuel combustion during dam construction
Jobs are created to maintain the dam Reservoir flood displaces human homes
No air pollutants released during electricity generation Algae grows in still-water producing methane
Control of downstream seasonal flooding Very costly

Wind Power

  Benefit   Con
Produces no air pollutants May kill birds or bats
Takes up little space Not always active (intermittency)
Can be installed at sea for higher energy yields Can be an eye-sore or noise distraction for humans
Can be used with farmlands to optimize land usage

Other solutions include pushing laws for higher fuel efficiencies (CAFE standards for miles per gallon), subsidizing tax credits for electric vehicles, charging stations, and hybrids, and switching to nuclear power (as it does not release as much greenhouse gases as burning fossil fuels does).
